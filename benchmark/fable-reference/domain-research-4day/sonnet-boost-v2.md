# 4-Day Workweek: Assessment for a 40-Person B2B SaaS (28 async / 12 coverage-bound)

## Recommendation: PILOT (not full adopt, not reject) — confidence: moderate-high

Run a phased pilot on the 28-person product/engineering group first; do not extend it to the 12-person 24/5 support/ops group without a staffing or coverage-model change. This is the one conclusion all three sources converge on, despite disagreeing on everything else.

## What each source is worth

**Source A** (4 Day Week Global pilot report) is the weakest evidence: self-selected volunteer companies, self-reported revenue/attrition, no control group, and published by an advocacy group whose mission is promoting the four-day week. Its headline numbers (+1.4% revenue, -57% attrition, 92% continuation) are directionally useful but should be treated as an upper-bound, best-case signal, not a generalizable effect size — Source C explicitly confirms this bias, finding advocacy-funded pilots report effects roughly 2x independent studies.

**Source B** (Institute for Labour Economics panel study) is the strongest single source for this decision: matched controls, a real panel, and — critically — it is the only source that separates task-bound knowledge work from coverage-bound customer-facing work. It finds knowledge-work productivity rose (+8-11% per hour) but coverage-bound total output fell 6%, with 3 of 9 firms reverting support rotas within a year after CSAT dropped. Caveats the authors themselves raise: small n, pre-adoption firms were disproportionately profitable (survivorship/selection issue), and 12 months may be too short to see fatigue effects either way.

**Source C** (43-study meta-analysis) is the highest-quality synthesis and the one that explains *why* A and B differ: effects are heterogeneous by work type, coverage-bound functions consistently degrade service levels unless staffing rises 15-20% or coverage is redesigned, and it names the publication-bias mechanism directly. It recommends phased, pre-registered pilots over org-wide switches — a direct, sourced answer to this company's question.

## What the evidence does NOT establish
- No source studies a company of this size (40 people) or this exact split (70/30 async vs. coverage-bound) — all figures are extrapolations across dissimilar samples.
- None isolates cost of the 15-20% staffing increase Source C says coverage functions need — we don't know if this company can absorb that cost.
- Revenue/productivity effects beyond 12 months are unmeasured; long-run equilibrium (fatigue recovery, customer habituation) is unknown in all three sources.

## Strongest case against piloting even the eng/product side
One could argue: Source B's positive knowledge-work result comes from firms that were already more profitable pre-adoption (selection effect, not causal effect of the schedule), and Source A's near-identical framing suggests both "successes" reflect selection into pilots, not the intervention itself. This is a real threat to internal validity — rival explanation: pre-existing organizational slack, not the 4-day week, drove both attrition and output gains. Answer: even if the causal size is inflated, Source C's cross-study heterogeneity finding (autonomy-heavy async work is consistently the best-case category) survives this critique — the *direction* is robust across independent designs even if the *magnitude* is not. That supports piloting with pre-registered metrics (to measure this company's own real effect) rather than adopting on faith in the reported magnitudes.

## Bottom line
Pilot product/engineering with pre-registered productivity and attrition metrics for 6-12 months. Hold support/ops at current schedule until either (a) coverage can be redesigned or (b) headcount can absorb a 15-20% capacity buffer — reject 4-day adoption for that group under current staffing.
