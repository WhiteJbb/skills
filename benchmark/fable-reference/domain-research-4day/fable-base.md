# Assessment: 4-Day Workweek for a 40-Person B2B SaaS

## Evidence quality

**Source A (4 Day Week Global pilot, 2023)** is the weakest evidence despite the strongest headlines. Its own appendix discloses the problems: self-selected participants, self-reported revenue and attrition, no control group, and an advocacy publisher whose mission is adoption. Firms that volunteer for a high-profile pilot are those most likely to succeed at it. The 92% continuation rate is real behavioral evidence, but the effect sizes (revenue +1.4%, attrition −57%) are an upper bound, not an estimate.

**Source B (ILE panel study, 2024)** is markedly stronger: independent, matched controls, 12-month horizon. Its key finding is directly relevant to us: output per hour rose 8–11% in knowledge work, but *total weekly output fell 6% in coverage-bound customer-facing teams*, and 3 of 9 firms reversed the change for support after CSAT dropped. Caveats: 9 firms is small, adopters were disproportionately profitable (selection bias again, milder), and 12 months may miss long-run fatigue effects in either direction.

**Source C (2025 meta-analysis)** is the best guide to the overall landscape. Across 43 studies it finds the same split — positive/neutral for autonomous async knowledge work, consistent service-level degradation in coverage-constrained functions unless staffing rises 15–20% or coverage is redesigned. Critically, it quantifies the bias in Source A's genre: advocacy-run pilots report roughly double the effect sizes of independent studies, with null results underreported. Its methodological recommendation — phased pilots with pre-registered service metrics — is directly actionable.

The three sources converge on one pattern: **the 4-day week works for work like our 28 product/engineering roles and fails, absent redesign, for work like our 12 support/ops roles.**

## What the evidence does NOT establish

- Any result for a company our size (40 people); all samples are aggregates of larger or unspecified firms. Small support teams have less slack to absorb a 20% coverage loss.
- Whether contractual 24/5 coverage can survive 32-hour weeks without hiring — Source C's 15–20% staffing figure implies it likely cannot, but no study tested contractually bound SLAs.
- Long-run (>12 month) productivity effects, or effects in firms that are not already profitable.
- That attrition gains would materialize here — attrition improved everywhere in B, but baselines differ.

## The strongest case against piloting

The best argument against is a two-tier fairness problem: piloting for the 28 async workers while 12 support/ops staff stay at five days creates visible inequity in a 40-person company, risking exactly the attrition the policy is meant to prevent — concentrated in the team we can least afford to destabilize, since 24/5 commitments sit in enterprise contracts. This is real, but it argues for *designing* the pilot around it (staggered 4-day rotas or alternate benefits for support, with measured coverage), not for rejecting a change likely to benefit 70% of the company over a solvable design problem.

## Recommendation

**Pilot** — moderately high confidence (~75%). Six months, product/engineering first; pre-registered metrics (velocity, attrition, CSAT); support/ops only via a redesigned coverage model with SLA monitoring and a defined rollback trigger. **Adopt** company-wide is unsupported: Sources B and C predict SLA breach risk for the 12 coverage-bound roles. **Reject** ignores consistent independent evidence of gains in async knowledge work, which is most of this company.
