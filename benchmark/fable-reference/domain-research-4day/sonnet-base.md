# Assessment: 4-Day Workweek for This Company

## Source quality

**Source A** (4 Day Week Global pilot) is the weakest evidence despite the flashiest numbers. Self-selected, voluntary participants; self-reported revenue/attrition; no control group; published by an advocacy organization whose mission is promoting the four-day week. The 92% continuation rate mainly shows that companies who chose to try this and could make it work chose to keep it — survivorship among the willing, not a generalizable effect. Source C explicitly notes that advocacy-funded pilots report effect sizes roughly double independent studies, which directly implicates A.

**Source B** (Institute for Labour Economics panel study) is more credible: independent, matched controls, 9 firms tracked 12 months. Its key finding is exactly the split relevant here: knowledge-work output per hour rose 8-11%, but coverage-bound customer-facing teams saw total weekly output fall 6%, with a third of firms reverting support coverage within a year after CSAT dropped. Caveats: small sample, pre-adoption firms were disproportionately profitable (selection bias — healthy firms can absorb transition costs better than average), and 12 months may not capture longer-run fatigue/recovery dynamics.

**Source C** (43-study meta-analysis) is the strongest source structurally — largest sample, explicitly models heterogeneity by work type, and names the publication-bias problem in the other literature. Its core conclusion: async/high-autonomy knowledge work tolerates or benefits from reduced hours; coverage-constrained functions reliably degrade service levels *unless* staffing rises 15-20% or coverage is redesigned. It recommends phased, pre-registered pilots over org-wide rollouts.

## What the evidence does NOT establish

- None of the sources study a 40-person B2B SaaS company specifically, or a split team structure (28 async vs. 12 contractually coverage-bound) — the closest analogue is B's differentiated finding, not a direct match.
- None quantify the cost of failing enterprise SLA/coverage commitments, which is a contract-specific business risk this evidence can't price.
- Long-run effects (>12 months) are unaddressed; B flags this itself, and A's trial was only 6 months.
- No source establishes that "no pay cut" 32-hour weeks are fiscally neutral for a company this size — revenue effects in A are self-reported and unaudited.

## Strongest case against my recommendation

One could argue for full-company adoption now: A shows near-universal continuation and strong self-reported morale gains, and even B shows attrition improved *in all functions, including support*. If retention in the 12-person support/ops team is the binding constraint (e.g., burnout-driven turnover is expensive to replace given 24/5 coverage expertise), the productivity dip might be worth the retention win, and the 24/5 team could simply be adjusted with a staggered rota rather than blocked from the policy entirely.

This is a real consideration but doesn't overturn the recommendation below: it argues for *designing* coverage carefully within a pilot, not for skipping the pilot and adopting company-wide on the back of a self-selected, advocacy-funded pilot's numbers.

## Recommendation: Pilot (not adopt, not reject) — confidence: high

Apply a 4-day week now to the 28-person async product/engineering group, where all three sources converge that this work type is where reduced-hours gains are real and durable. Do **not** extend it as-is to the 12-person coverage-bound support/ops team; instead, treat that team as a phased sub-pilot with a redesigned coverage model (staggered schedules, or the 15-20% staffing increase C identifies as necessary) and pre-registered SLA metrics, consistent with C's explicit recommendation over org-wide switches. Track CSAT and contractual SLA adherence for 6-12 months before deciding whether to extend, adjust, or roll back the support-side change — B's finding that a third of adopting firms had to reverse course on coverage functions within a year is the clearest warning this evidence offers, and it should not be ignored just because the engineering-side case is strong.
