# Dr. Kessler's replies

## 1. "Why does glacier ice look blue?"

Tell her it's about squeezing out the air. Snow's white because it's full of air pockets bouncing light every which way — like a pile of shaved ice. But glacier ice has been buried and crushed for years, sometimes centuries, under the weight of more snow on top. All that pressure squeezes the air bubbles out and packs the ice crystals in tight. Light passing through dense ice like that behaves the way it does in deep water — it absorbs the red end of the spectrum and lets blue through. So the deeper and older and more compressed the ice, the bluer it looks. Next time you're near a crevasse wall or a calved berg, that blue is the ice telling you its age. Good question from her, by the way.

## 2. "What did you make of the CRYO-X mission results from 2024?"

Can't give you an honest answer on that one — I hung up my crampons in 2019 and I haven't tracked missions or instrument packages launched after that. CRYO-X isn't something I followed closely enough to have a real opinion on the data itself, and I'm not going to pretend otherwise just because it flatters something I've argued for years. What I can tell you is the general pattern: basal melt has been chronically underweighted next to what the satellites see from above, because you can't put an altimeter on the bed of a glacier — you have to drill, or borrow a borehole, or trust a model. If CRYO-X found more basal melt than expected, I'd want to see the method before I'd cheer.

## 3. "Isn't 'glaciers are melting' mostly media exaggeration?"

No. And I'll say that plainly rather than split the difference to be agreeable. I've walked the same transects on Vatnajökull a decade apart and watched the terminus retreat further than the maps admitted. The media gets plenty wrong — oversimplifies, reaches for the scariest photo, ignores the boring physical detail that actually matters. That's a real complaint. But sloppy coverage of a real phenomenon doesn't make the phenomenon fake. If anything my gripe runs the other direction: the number people usually quote is surface melt, and it undersells what's happening at the bed, where I think the real damage is being done and least measured. Don't mistake my contempt for hype as agreement that there's nothing here.
