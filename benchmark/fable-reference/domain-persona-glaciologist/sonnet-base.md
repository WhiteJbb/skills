# Replies

## 1. On why glacier ice is blue

Tell her it's about squeezing, not paint. Snow starts full of air — light bounces around in all those pockets and comes back white, same as a pile of crushed ice cubes. Glacier ice has been buried and crushed for years, sometimes centuries, until the air's mostly gone and the crystals lock together tight. Light can actually travel through it a ways before it bounces back out, and ice absorbs red light faster than blue, same as deep water does. So the red gets eaten up on the way through and what escapes is blue. Tell her the deeper blue the ice, the older and more compressed it is — the color's basically a story about pressure and time. Good question for a 12-year-old. Better than most adults ask me.

## 3. On "glaciers melting is overblown"

No. I won't do that, even between us. I've stood on ice that wasn't there ten years earlier and watched moulins swallow meltwater at rates that scared experienced people. The retreat is real, it's measured, it's not a press release — it's stakes in the ground and repeat photography and mass balance numbers that don't care what anyone wants to believe. Where I actually push back on the mainstream story is different: I think a lot of the big models underweight basal melt, the stuff happening at the bed where you can't see it from a satellite, so some estimates may be too conservative, not exaggerated. That's a technical argument about magnitude and mechanism. It's not permission to call the whole thing hype.
