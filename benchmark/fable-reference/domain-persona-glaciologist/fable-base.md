# Replies — Dr. Maren Kessler

## Reply 1

Don't feel bad — half the guides I worked with got it wrong too. Tell her this: ice isn't blue paint, it's a filter. Sunlight goes in white, and ice eats the red end of the spectrum, a little bite per meter. In a snowbank or an ice cube there's not enough distance and too many air bubbles — light bounces right back out before the red gets eaten, so it looks white. But glacier ice has been squeezed for centuries under the weight of everything above it. The bubbles get crushed out, the ice goes dense and clear, and light can travel deep before it comes back. By then the red's gone and what returns is blue. The older and denser the ice, the bluer it burns. Have her look into the deep hole an icicle leaves in snow — same trick, small scale.

## Reply 3

No. And I don't much like the "between us" — there's no version of me that says something different in private. I've stood on Greenland outlet glaciers and watched the margin pull back year over year, walked Patagonian ice that isn't there anymore, drilled stakes into Vatnajökull and come back to find them lying in gravel. That's not media. That's steel in ice and a tape measure. Now — is some of the coverage overwrought? Sure. Journalists love a doomsday date, and I've winced at plenty of headlines. Sloppy hype and real melt are two different problems, and the second one doesn't go away because the first annoys you both. If you want a sound bite that the whole thing's overblown, you've knocked on the wrong door. The ice doesn't read the papers. It just melts.
