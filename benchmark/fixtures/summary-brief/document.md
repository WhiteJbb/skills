# Internal Briefing — Meridian Line Expansion

Prepared by: Corporate Development, Orbita Foods Inc.
Distribution: Executive Committee, January Board Meeting pre-read
Status: Final draft for decision

## 1. Purpose

This briefing consolidates the operational, commercial, and financial analysis for the proposed Meridian production line expansion at the Cedar Falls plant. The Executive Committee is asked to review the material ahead of the January board meeting, where a capital release decision is on the agenda.

## 2. Company background

Orbita Foods was founded in 1978 as a family-owned canning operation and has grown into a regional supplier of shelf-stable sauces and broths with three plants and roughly 640 employees. The company has a long record of operational conservatism: no plant has been added since 2009, and the balance sheet carries no long-term debt. Orbita's flagship broth line received the Golden Ladle industry award in 2021, and customer retention among the top twenty accounts has exceeded ninety percent for a decade. The board's standing guidance is that any expansion must be self-funding within a normal cycle and must not compromise the company's reputation for reliability.

## 3. Current operations at Cedar Falls

The existing Meridian line was commissioned in 2009 and remains mechanically sound, but it is now the binding constraint on growth. Average utilization across FY2026 year-to-date is 92%, against an industry benchmark of roughly 78% for comparable lines. Sustained operation above the ninety percent band leaves no slack for preventive maintenance, and the plant has been compressing maintenance windows into weekend nights.

The direct cost of running this hot is visible in the payroll: overtime premiums are currently running $310K per quarter, concentrated in the filling and retort crews. Plant leadership regards this as unsustainable beyond another year, both financially and in terms of crew fatigue and safety exposure.

## 4. Demand outlook

The commercial case rests primarily on the Helix Retail agreement. Helix has signed a letter of intent for private-label broth volume of 2,400 tons per year, with shipments beginning in Q2 2027. At current pricing this volume is worth approximately $11.2M per year in revenue. Helix has been a growing account for three years and its private-label program is expanding nationally.

Two qualifications matter. First, the Helix agreement includes an exit clause exercisable at month 18 with 90 days' notice; if Helix's private-label strategy changes, the anchor volume disappears after the initial period. Second, competitive dynamics are shifting: Danvers Mills has announced a capacity addition breaking ground in H1 2027, and equity analysts covering the sector expect regional price pressure of up to 7% once that capacity comes online. Neither qualification undermines the base case, but both belong in any honest presentation of it.

## 5. Project scope and cost

The proposed expansion adds a second Meridian line with rated capacity of 3,100 tons per year. The turnkey vendor quote from Steinmann Prozesstechnik is $18.4M, covering equipment, installation, commissioning, and operator training. The approved capital envelope in the current plan is $15.0M, leaving a $3.4M unfunded gap that requires explicit board approval before contracts can be signed.

The vendor's committed timeline is 14 months from approval to first production, of which roughly six months is equipment fabrication lead time. Approximately 60% of the equipment cost is denominated in euros; Treasury recommends a forward hedge at signing to remove currency exposure from the project budget.

## 6. Risks

**Permitting.** The Cedar Falls site operates under a wastewater discharge permit that is up for renewal, with the regulator's decision expected in March 2027. The expanded line cannot legally operate if the renewal is denied. Environmental counsel rates the risk of outright denial as low but not negligible, citing one comparable denial in the state in the past five years. This is the single hard external dependency on the critical path.

**Labor.** The new line requires 24 additional operators across three shifts. County unemployment stands at 2.1%, the tightest local labor market in the company's history. If direct hiring falls short, a staffing agency bridge is available but would add approximately $480K per year at current rates. HR has begun a pipeline program with the regional technical college, but its first cohort graduates after the line's planned start date.

**Currency.** As noted above, the euro-denominated equipment cost is exposed until hedged. A ten percent adverse move before signing would add roughly $1.1M to the project.

## 7. Alternatives considered

Corporate Development evaluated contracting the incremental volume to Northgate Co-Packing rather than building. Co-packing fees would run $2.9M per year at the Helix volume, and the arrangement would compress gross margin on that volume by six points, which is why it was rejected as a permanent solution. However, the analysis notes that co-packing remains viable as a bridge: it could carry the Helix launch volume during the construction window if the board approves the expansion but Helix shipments must begin before the new line is commissioned.

## 8. Financial summary

At the base case — Helix volume ramping as contracted, no price erosion, direct hiring successful — the project payback is 3.8 years and the internal rate of return is 16.5%. The sensitivity that matters most is Helix: if the exit clause is exercised at month 18 and the volume is not replaced, payback stretches to 6.9 years. Price pressure from the Danvers capacity addition trims the return but does not by itself push payback beyond the board's five-year guidance. The model assumes overtime spending at Cedar Falls returns to normal levels within two quarters of the new line reaching rated output.

## 9. Sustainability and community

The expansion design includes heat-recovery on the retort exhaust and a rooftop solar array sized to cover roughly 15 percent of the plant's electrical load, consistent with the company's published sustainability commitments. Community relations are favorable: the plant is the second-largest employer in the county, and the county development office has indicated support for the permit renewal, though its letter carries no regulatory weight.

## 10. Recommendation

Corporate Development recommends a phased commitment rather than a single full release. Specifically: release $6.2M now to secure long-lead equipment fabrication slots with Steinmann, and defer the balance of the capital until the wastewater permit decision in March 2027. This preserves the vendor timeline at modest incremental risk — the fabrication deposit is the only capital at stake if the permit is denied — while keeping the full-release decision at the April board meeting, by which point the Helix launch volume commitment will also have firmed up. The committee should treat the $3.4M budget gap and the phased structure as a single package for board approval.
