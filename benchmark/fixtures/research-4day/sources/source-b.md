# Excerpt B — "Compressed workweeks and output: a 12-month panel study" (Institute for Labour Economics working paper, 2024)

Independent panel study of 9 firms (1,140 employees) that adopted 32-hour weeks, with matched-control comparison firms. Findings: output per hour worked rose 8-11% across knowledge-work functions, but total weekly output fell 6% on average in customer-facing teams (support, success, field sales), where work is coverage-bound rather than task-bound. Three of the nine firms reinstated five-day coverage rotas for support functions within the year after customer-satisfaction scores dropped. Attrition improved in all functions. The authors caution that the sample is small, adopting firms were disproportionately profitable pre-adoption, and 12 months may be too short to observe fatigue-recovery equilibria.
